What if there's a lost chapter of Gilgamesh in a museum drawer?

The Epic of Gilgamesh was pieced together from dozens of tablets found across different sites over more than a century. New fragments are still being identified. The most recent major addition was discovered in 2015.

There could be more. We won't know until we look.

What observations did Babylonian astronomers record that we've never seen?

Ancient Mesopotamians tracked celestial events for centuries. Their records include observations of comets, eclipses, and planetary movements that modern astronomers use to calibrate historical chronologies.

Thousands of astronomical texts remain unread in collections.

Whose letter home never got answered?

Among the tablets are personal letters: merchants writing to family, soldiers to loved ones, children to parents. Complaints about business partners, requests for supplies, expressions of worry.

These voices from the past are as human as anything written today.

Why They Remain Unread

Transcribing cuneiform is painstaking work requiring specialized expertise. At the current pace, processing the full backlog would take centuries. Most tablets have sat untouched for decades.

A New Path Forward

1

AI does the first pass

Machine learning identifies and suggests readings for individual signs, handling the mechanical work.

2

Contributors verify visually

Anyone can compare suggested readings against tablet images. No expertise required - just pattern matching.

3

Consensus builds confidence

Multiple independent verifications create reliable readings. Statistical methods identify when agreement is strong.

4

Scholars validate and interpret

Experts review transcriptions, correct errors, and unlock the meaning. The hard work remains scholarly.
