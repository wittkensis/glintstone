Beneath museum glass, half a million voices wait in silence

Ancient Mesopotamians recorded their laws, their loves, their recipes, and their fears on clay tablets. Most have never been read by modern eyes.

The Scale of What Remains

More than 500,000 cuneiform tablets sit in museum collections around the world. They represent the earliest chapters of written human history, spanning over three millennia of Mesopotamian civilization.

There are approximately 200 active Assyriologists working today. At the current pace of academic scholarship, processing this backlog would take centuries.

What These Tablets Contain
Administrative
Tax records, inventories, worker rosters, and trade accounts that reveal the economics of ancient empires
Literary
Epic poetry, hymns, proverbs, and stories including versions of texts that predate the Hebrew Bible
Scientific
Medical treatments, astronomical observations, and mathematical calculations that influenced later civilizations
Personal
Letters between family members, marriage contracts, and complaints to authorities that humanize the ancient world
A New Approach

Glintstone combines artificial intelligence with human expertise to accelerate cuneiform transcription. Machine learning handles initial sign recognition, while contributors verify and refine the results.

This is not a replacement for scholarly work. It is a tool to help scholars and institutions process what would otherwise remain unread for generations.
