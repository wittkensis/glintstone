Background

Rachel listens to history podcasts during her commute and reads popular history books on weekends. She's fascinated by ancient civilizations but has no interest in contributing to research—she just wants to learn.

She discovered Glintstone's contextualized "Grokipedia" articles about specific tablets and finds them more engaging than Wikipedia. The human stories revealed in ancient documents captivate her—4,000-year-old letters from worried parents, business disputes, love poems.

Time Budget

Daily reading 20-30 min (commute)
Weekend deep dives 1-2 hrs/week
Contribution appetite Zero

Primary Goals

  1. Learn interesting stories from ancient tablets

    Wants narrative, not academic analysis. Human interest angles, surprising revelations, relatable moments.

  2. Understand daily life 4,000 years ago

    What did people eat? Worry about? Joke about? The mundane is fascinating when it's ancient.

  3. Follow along as new tablets are translated

    Wants to feel connected to ongoing discovery. "Breaking news" from ancient Mesopotamia.

Key Needs from the System

  • Readable content

    Engaging prose without academic jargon. Written for interested laypeople, not specialists.

  • Rich context

    Historical era, geographic location, human stories. Help her understand what life was like.

  • Discovery paths

    "Related tablets" and thematic collections. She wants to fall down rabbit holes.

  • Accessibility

    Content works on mobile, audio-friendly for podcast-like consumption during commute.

Pain Points & Frustrations

  • Academic gatekeeping

    Content written for scholars is inaccessible. Jargon, assumptions, and dry prose lose her.

  • Scattered information

    Finding good content about specific tablets requires visiting multiple sites. Wants one source.

  • Static content

    Wikipedia doesn't update when new discoveries happen. Wants living, evolving knowledge.

  • Pressure to contribute

    Some citizen science sites make her feel guilty for just reading. She's here to learn, not work.
