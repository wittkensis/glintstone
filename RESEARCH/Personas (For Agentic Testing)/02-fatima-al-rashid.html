Background

Dr. Al-Rashid has spent 28 years studying Neo-Assyrian royal inscriptions. She has published extensively in major journals (ZA, JCS, NABU), served on the CDLI advisory board, and collated tablets at the British Museum, Louvre, and Vorderasiatisches Museum. She personally knows most of the ~100 specialists in her subfield.

She is skeptical of AI but open to tools that genuinely accelerate her work. Her primary concern is that AI "hallucinations" could corrupt the scholarly record if not caught. She's seen promising digital humanities projects come and go, and will only invest time in platforms that demonstrate staying power and academic rigor.

Primary Goals

  1. Accelerate her own research pipeline

    Wants AI to generate first-draft transcriptions she can correct, potentially 5-10x faster than working from scratch.

  2. Ensure quality control on AI-assisted content

    Feels responsible for preventing hallucinated content from corrupting the scholarly record.

  3. Maintain attribution standards

    Attribution isn't vanity—it's how scholarship works. Her career depends on proper credit.

  4. Train the next generation effectively

    Concerned about deskilling if students rely on AI without understanding. Wants pedagogically sound tools.

Key Needs from the System

  • Integration with existing tools

    Must export to ATF format, sync with CDLI P-numbers, and work alongside Oracc.

  • Granular attribution

    Every contribution type tracked separately: transcription, translation, collation, review.

  • Review authority

    Clear mechanisms to accept, reject, or modify proposed content. Expert judgment respected.

  • Transparent AI disclosure

    Must know when content is AI-generated, with what model, and with what confidence.

  • Scholarly credibility signals

    Advisory board of respected scholars, institutional partnerships, peer endorsements.

Pain Points & Frustrations

  • AI hallucinations

    Plausible-sounding but fabricated translations could propagate through citations if not caught.

  • Loss of nuance

    AI systems may flatten the uncertainty and ambiguity that characterizes scholarship.

  • Deskilling risk

    If researchers rely on AI, will philological training atrophy? Will future scholars evaluate AI critically?

  • Attribution confusion

    How should AI contributions be credited? If AI generates a draft humans revise, who is the author?

  • Platform abandonment

    Has seen promising digital humanities projects disappear after funding ends.
